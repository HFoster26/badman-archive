The Badman Tradition

The badman is a figure from African American folklore — a man who stands outside the law, whose community knows his name, and whose story gets told and retold because of what he represents, not because of what he owns. This page explains who the archive is looking at and how we decide who gets in.

Where the tradition comes from

The badman shows up in songs, stories, and reputation long before he shows up in books. Stagolee, Railroad Bill, Devil Winston — these are the nineteenth-century figures whose deeds were sung in work camps, juke joints, and prison yards across the South. The tradition traveled north with the Great Migration, arriving in Detroit and other industrial cities with the people who carried it. What began as oral folklore in rural communities reshaped itself around the factories, the streets, and the political struggles of the twentieth-century city.

What makes a badman

The archive evaluates every figure against five criteria. A high badman score doesn't mean a person is "good" or "bad" in a moral sense — it means their story matches the shape of the tradition. A figure can score high on some criteria and low on others; that's how modality assignment works. The five criteria are:

1. Outlaw relationship

How does the figure stand in relation to official authority? A badman is not defined by criminality — a revolutionary leader and a street hustler both stand outside institutional authority, in different ways. The outlaw relationship is the hinge the rest of the criteria turn on.

2. Community authorization

Does the community the figure comes from recognize him as one of their own? A badman whose people have disowned him is a different kind of figure than a badman whose neighborhood protects his legend. The archive weighs community authorization heavily because the badman tradition is, at its root, a community-authored tradition.

3. Violence as language

What role does force — physical, rhetorical, organizational — play in the figure's story? Violence in the badman tradition isn't always literal. A revolutionary's speech can carry the weight of a threat. A superhero's powers carry the same weight in a different form. The criterion asks how the figure wields force, not whether he is violent.

4. Cultural preservation

Does the figure's story preserve something the community is at risk of losing — a memory, a practice, a way of moving through the world? A badman who documents his neighborhood as it's being destroyed is doing preservation work whether he calls it that or not. This criterion asks what the figure keeps alive.

5. Hypermasculine performance

How does the figure perform his masculinity, and what does that performance do? The badman tradition has always been gendered — the archive documents Black male figures specifically because that is the tradition it is trying to capture. The criterion asks how that masculinity is staged: through the body, the voice, the outfit, the reputation, the refusal to be moved.

The modalities

Every figure is assigned to one of five modalities based on the shape of his story. The modalities are analytical, not moral — they describe how a figure fits the tradition, not whether he is a "good" or "bad" person.

Detective

Figures who work a case, read a city, and bring a reading of events back to a community that needs to know. Detroit's detective tradition runs from Action Jackson to Amos Walker to Lincoln Keller.

Revolutionary

Figures whose outlaw status comes from political action — organizers, party leaders, and movement figures whose confrontation with the state is ideological, not criminal. Kenyatta, General Gordon Baker Jr., the Obadele brothers, and Ron Scott belong here.

Superhero-Villain

Figures from comics, film, and television whose powers operate the way violence-as-language operates in the tradition: as a mode of speech rather than just a mode of force. Static, Hardware, Cyborg, Firestorm, and Blade anchor Detroit's presence in the superhero-villain modality.

Gangsta-Pimp (pending)

The modality documented most extensively in Detroit by Donald Goines. Figures whose relationship to the extractive economy mirrors the postindustrial conditions around them. Scheduled to go live in a future phase.

Folk Hero-Outlaw (pending)

The oldest modality in the tradition and the one most directly inherited from nineteenth-century folklore. Reserved for future expansion.
