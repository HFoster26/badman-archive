The Project

The Detroit Badman Digital Archive is a digital humanities project documenting Black masculine heroism in Detroit across real life, fiction, film, and television. It is built at Michigan State University's Cultural Heritage Informatics Fellowship and serves as the proof-of-concept for a national multi-city archive.

What the archive is

The archive documents figures whose stories shape Detroit's cultural memory — detectives, revolutionaries, and superhero-villains at launch, with gangsta-pimp and folk hero-outlaw modalities joining as the project grows. Every figure is documented through six structured categories: identity, a five-criteria badman score, biography, geography, network connections, and primary sources. The result is a view of Detroit's cultural record that traditional institutional archives miss — the layer of narrative and reputation that lives in stories, not documents.

Who runs it

The archive is directed by Harry M. Foster, a doctoral candidate in the Department of English at Michigan State University. Development is supported by MSU's Cultural Heritage Informatics Fellowship. The archive's long-term institutional home will transition to Silent Star Media following the dissertation phase.

Community partnerships with Detroit cultural institutions — the Charles H. Wright Museum, the Walter P. Reuther Library, and the Detroit Public Library's E. Azalia Hackley Collection — provide the institutional grounding that a single-researcher project cannot on its own.

What it is trying to do

Three goals drive the archive. First, to document Detroit's badman figures in a form that centers community narrative alongside scholarly documentation. Second, to build a framework — data model, visual identity, review pipeline — that can be ported to other cities without rebuilding from scratch. Third, to treat community members as sources of knowledge rather than subjects of study, through a submission pipeline that invites Detroit residents to contribute the stories they already tell each other.

Why Detroit first

Detroit produces badman figures at a density few cities match. Detectives like Amos Walker and Action Jackson; revolutionaries like General Gordon Baker Jr. and the Obadele brothers; superhero-villains like Static, Hardware, Cyborg, and Firestorm; and a literary tradition anchored by Donald Goines that shaped a half-century of Black street fiction. The city's badman production is dense enough across modalities to test whether the archive's framework holds.

National expansion

Detroit is the first city, not the only one. Atlanta, New Orleans, Washington D.C., Memphis, Chicago, New York, Philadelphia, Los Angeles, the badmen of Texas, and Miami's Afro-Caribbean badman tradition are the expansion roadmap. Each city will get its own data file, its own community partnerships, and its own curated figures — but will share this archive's data model, scoring framework, and review pipeline.
