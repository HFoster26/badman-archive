Biography

Virgil Ovid Hawkins is a 15-year-old Black teenager from the Paris Island neighborhood of Dakota City — a fictional Midwestern city explicitly modeled on Detroit by creator Dwayne McDuffie. Brilliant, motor-mouthed, and geeky, Virgil gains electromagnetic superpowers during the Big Bang, a mass-empowerment event caused when police deploy experimental Alva Industries tear gas on gang members (in the original comics) or BLM protesters (in the 2021 revival). In every version, police action is the catalyst — state violence against Black communities generates the very crisis authorities then seek to control. As Static, Virgil protects his working-class neighborhoods against Bang Baby villains, corporate predator Edwin Alva, and the institutional systems that created the disaster. The Static Shock animated series (2000-2004) made him the first Black superhero to headline a broadcast animated series, reaching millions of viewers and establishing him as a generational icon.

Timeline

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 4 / 5
Vigilante teenager operating entirely outside law enforcement. Police CAUSED the Big Bang — deploying experimental Alva Industries chemicals against gang members (original) or tear-gassing BLM protesters (2021 revival). Every continuity positions cops as antagonists or catalysts of the crisis. In Shadows of Dakota (2023), Virgil intervenes when police harass homeless people. Refuses to register or cooperate with authorities.
Community Authorization 5 / 5
Protects Paris Island and Sadler — his own working-class Black neighborhoods. His father Robert works at Paris Island Hospital. His community center, school, and family anchor him. The animated series (2000-2004, 52 episodes, Phil LaMarr) made him the most recognized Black superhero for an entire generation of children. McDuffie said Static 'could easily have been me at that age, if I'd had super powers.'
Violence as Language 4 / 5
Electromagnetic powers deployed decisively — but Virgil is a teenager whose first instinct is wit and intelligence, not force. He talks his way through confrontations when possible. Physical violence is reactive and protective. His restraint distinguishes him from Hotstreak, his foil, who is pure destructive force. The detective's restraint principle applies: a kid who can electrocute anyone but reads the room instead.
Cultural Preservation Function 4 / 5
The animated series tackled gun violence (won 2003 Humanitas Prize), racism, homelessness, bullying. McDuffie built Dakota from Detroit's economic dissolution — preserving the city's narrative in fictional form. Static #25's condom controversy was itself a preservation act — insisting on depicting Black teenage sexuality honestly against corporate resistance. DC's blog: 'Virgil was named after Virgil Hawkins, the black man who was denied entrance into the University of Florida law school.'
Hypermasculine Performance 4 / 5
Teenage masculinity — smart-mouthed, brave, physically capable but not physically dominant. His power is electricity, not muscle. Masculinity expressed through courage, loyalty, and intellectual confidence rather than physical intimidation. He's the geek who can fight, not the fighter who reads.

Total score: 21 / 25 (5 criteria scored)
