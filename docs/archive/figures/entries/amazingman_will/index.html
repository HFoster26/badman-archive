Biography

William Blake Everett was born in Decatur, Mississippi, to a family of sharecroppers. After young Will defied a white landowner, the family migrated to Detroit, where his father found work at an auto plant — a fictional rendering of the Great Migration that brought millions of Black Southerners to Northern industrial cities. Will grew up in Detroit, excelled in athletics, and competed in the 1936 Berlin Olympics, winning gold alongside Jesse Owens. He returned to a nation that celebrated his athletic achievement but denied his humanity. The Ultra-Humanite later kidnapped and experimented on Everett, granting him the power of molecular mimicry — the ability to take on the physical properties of any material he touches. Initially forced to serve the villain, Everett broke free and joined the All-Star Squadron. In All-Star Squadron #38-40, the team traveled to Detroit to help Everett fight 'The Real American,' a bigoted villain leading the Phantom Empire — a KKK-like organization terrorizing Black Detroiters. Everett was arrested and nearly lynched before being rescued. Post-war, he became one of the most important civil rights leaders in the DC Universe, leading anti-segregation marches in Detroit for decades.

Timeline

  2. 1942 Kidnapped by the Ultra-Humanite and subjected to experiments granting molecular mimicry powers. Breaks free and joins the All-Star Squadron. — Various (fictional WWII setting)
  3. 1942 All-Star Squadron #38-40: The team travels to Detroit. Everett fights the Phantom Empire (KKK analog) terrorizing Black residents. He is arrested and nearly lynched before fighting back. — Detroit, MI (fictional)
  4. 1983 First real-world appearance in All-Star Squadron #23 (July 1983). Created by Roy Thomas and Jerry Ordway to address DC's absence of Black WWII-era heroes. — Publication — DC Comics

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 4 / 5
Gained his powers through Nazi experimentation against his will — the Ultra-Humanite used him as a test subject. Initially forced to serve as a villain's agent before breaking free. His relationship to American law is defined by the fact that 1940s American law did not recognize him as fully human. An Olympic gold medalist who came home to Jim Crow. The law he is asked to defend never defended him.
Community Authorization 4 / 5
Jesse Owens's fictional counterpart — a Black Olympian who became a superhero. Led civil rights marches in Detroit post-war, described as the third most important civil rights advocate in the DC Universe. His community authorization is earned through decades of activism, not just superheroics. His grandson Will Everett III inherited his powers and Detroit roots.
Violence as Language 4 / 5
Molecular mimicry — can take on the properties of any material he touches: steel skin, diamond fists, liquid form. His power is literalized adaptation. Violence is defensive and protective. In All-Star Squadron #38-40, he fights the Phantom Empire (KKK analog) in Detroit to protect Black residents being terrorized. He was nearly lynched before fighting back. The violence he deploys was first deployed against him.
Cultural Preservation Function 4 / 5
His origin IS the Great Migration narrative — sharecropper family from Decatur, Mississippi to Detroit auto plants after young Will defied a white landowner. His Berlin Olympics backstory preserves the Jesse Owens legacy in fictional form. His post-war civil rights activism documents the continuity between WWII service and the civil rights movement. Roy Thomas created him explicitly to fill a historical gap in DC's representation.
Hypermasculine Performance 3 / 5
Olympic athlete — the physical credential is there. But Everett's masculinity is expressed through endurance and moral courage rather than dominance. He survives lynching, Nazi experimentation, and systemic racism. His performance is survival itself — the Black man who absorbs whatever the world throws at him and transforms it into strength. Literally — his power is to become whatever he touches.
