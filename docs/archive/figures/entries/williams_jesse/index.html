Biography

Sgt. Jesse Williams is a Black police sergeant on the Detroit Police Department, played by Hari Rhodes in the 1973 film Detroit 9000. A former college football hero and the refined, intellectual half of an interracial detective partnership, Williams is paired with Lt. Danny Bassett (Alex Rocco) to investigate the theft of $400,000 from a congressional fundraiser at the Book Cadillac Hotel supporting Congressman Aubrey Hale Clayton's gubernatorial campaign. The case plunges Williams into a web of political corruption, prostitution, fencing operations, and murder that crosses racial lines — the masked robbers deliberately concealed their race using a pre-recorded tape. When Williams's college sweetheart Roby Harris (Vonetta McGee) is killed in his arms, the investigation becomes personal. The film climaxes in shootouts at an abandoned railroad station and Elmwood Cemetery, and concludes on a cruise ship where Williams discovers his partner Danny may have been trying to steal the loot rather than arrest the fence. Danny dies without answering, leaving Williams to deliver the film's iconic closing line. Williams is the moral survivor — the Black detective left standing to contemplate the impossibility of clean justice in a corrupt system.

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 3 / 5
Works within the system as a Detroit police sergeant. But the system assigns him a corrupt partner, and his moral clarity puts him in tension with institutional priorities. He survives by reading the system, not serving it. His final moral judgment — ambiguity about whether his partner was the best or worst cop — represents the detective's refusal to accept the system's categories.
Community Authorization 3 / 5
Protects a Black congressional campaign's integrity against robbery and political sabotage. Connected to his community through his girlfriend (university professor) and college sweetheart Roby Harris. But his authorization comes primarily from the institutional badge rather than grassroots community recognition.
Violence as Language 5 / 5
Former college football hero and armed police sergeant. Participates in shootouts at Elmwood Cemetery and the train station. Shoots the officer on the cruise ship in the climactic confrontation. Physical violence is central to the film's action sequences. Williams uses decisive force when detection requires it — the detective who reads first and shoots when the reading is done.
Cultural Preservation Function 3 / 5
The film itself preserves 1973 Detroit as an irreplaceable visual time capsule — now-demolished landmarks (Hudson's building, Fort Street Station), real Detroit personalities (Police Chief John Nichols, Martha Jean 'The Queen' Steinberg), and Holland-Dozier-Holland music. Williams as a character doesn't have an explicit preservation function, but the film's on-location Detroit shooting constitutes cultural documentation.
Hypermasculine Performance 4 / 5
Former college football hero. Physically commanding. The educated, refined Black detective — masculinity performed through intellectual superiority and physical capability simultaneously. He is the moral survivor of the film: the one left standing while his white partner dies. His final line is not bravado but moral seriousness.
