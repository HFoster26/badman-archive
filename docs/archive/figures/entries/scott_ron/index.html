Biography

Ron Scott grew up in Detroit's Jeffries Projects, where his politically astute single mother shaped his worldview. Accepted to the University of Michigan in 1965 before affirmative action, Scott's trajectory shifted after hearing Kathleen Cleaver speak at St. Matthew/St. Joseph Church, leading him to co-found Detroit's Black Panther Party chapter in 1968. For nearly five decades, Scott remained on the front lines of Detroit's struggle for justice—from Panther organizing to police accountability advocacy to community peace-building. As spokesperson for the Detroit Coalition Against Police Brutality and founder of Peace Zones for Life, Scott evolved from revolutionary militant to 'Transformational Anthropologist,' as he called himself, dedicated to changing human behavior toward peace and reconciliation. He died of cancer on November 29, 2015, working until his final days.

Community Authorization 5 / 5

Violence as Language 4 / 5
Armed self-defense was central to Panther ideology. However, Scott's later work emphasized de-escalation and peace-building, representing evolution from violent resistance to community reconciliation.
Cultural Preservation Function 5 / 5
Preserved and transmitted Black radical tradition through decades of organizing, media work, and mentorship. Hosted 'For My People' on Detroit PBS affiliate. Participated in Detroit Black Journal. Educated new generations about Panther legacy.

Embodied revolutionary Black masculinity as Panther leader. Public speaker and confrontational presence at police commission meetings. However, later work emphasized community healing over masculine dominance.
