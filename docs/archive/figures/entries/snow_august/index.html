Biography

August Octavio Snow is a biracial detective — half African American, half Mexican American — operating in Detroit's Mexicantown neighborhood. Created by Stephen Mack Jones, Snow is a former Marine sniper and former Detroit Police Department officer who uncovered corruption at the highest levels of city government. Corrupt cops and politicians conspired to drum him out of the department, but he fought back and won a $12 million wrongful dismissal settlement. After a year away, he returned to his childhood home on fictional West Markham Street in Mexicantown, using his wealth to buy and renovate houses in the neighborhood — rebuilding his community one property at a time. Across four novels, Snow confronts corporate embezzlement and cyber-terrorism (August Snow), human trafficking preying on undocumented immigrants (Lives Laid Away), gentrification threatening to rebrand Mexicantown as 'East Corktown' (Dead of Winter), and a Catholic Church cover-up (Deus X). His father was a Black Detroit cop. His mother was a Mexican American painter who read him Spanish-language poets. Snow does not see a cultural dichotomy in himself — he got the best of what both cultures offer.

Timeline

  1. 2017 First appearance in August Snow. Returns to Mexicantown after winning $12M wrongful dismissal settlement against DPD. Investigates death of business magnate Eleanore Paget. — Detroit, MI / Grosse Pointe, MI (fictional)
  2. 2019 Lives Laid Away published. Body of young Hispanic woman dredged from Detroit River. Snow uncovers human trafficking scheme targeting undocumented immigrants in Mexicantown. — Detroit, MI (fictional)
  3. 2021 Dead of Winter published. Authentico Foods Inc., a 30-year Mexicantown tortilla business, is blackmailed by anonymous billionaire developer. Snow fights gentrification. — Detroit, MI (fictional)
  4. 2023 Deus X published. Father Grabowski, Mexicantown's 40-year Franciscan priest, suddenly retires. Snow investigates Catholic Church cover-up involving religious secret societies. — Detroit, MI (fictional)

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 4 / 5
Former DPD officer expelled by systemic corruption who fought back and won a $12 million wrongful dismissal settlement. Operates outside law enforcement but alongside it. His wealth gives him independence the system cannot leverage. Not anti-law but ungovernable by it — the detective who loved being a cop, was betrayed by the institution, and now operates in the space between.
Community Authorization 4 / 5
Uses his settlement money to buy and renovate houses in Mexicantown — literally rebuilding the community property by property. Protects undocumented immigrants against ICE raids and human trafficking. Fights gentrification developers threatening to rebrand Mexicantown as 'East Corktown.' Community allies include Father Grabowski (40-year Mexicantown priest) and Elena (immigrant advocate). His community is Mexicantown's Mexican American population as much as Detroit's Black community.
Violence as Language 5 / 5
Retired Marine sniper with a distinguished military career — trained killer at the highest level. Former DPD officer. Physical violence is decisive and frequent across all four novels: shootouts, hand-to-hand combat, tactical operations. Also deploys legal force ($12M lawsuit), economic force (property acquisition), and investigative force. Violence is one tool in a sophisticated arsenal wielded by a man whose restraint is itself a form of power.
Cultural Preservation Function 4 / 5
Preserves Mexicantown's physical and cultural infrastructure against gentrification. The novels document Southwest Detroit's working-class multiethnic reality — food, language, churches, businesses, immigrant networks. Jones's Kresge Arts in Detroit Literary Fellowship recognizes this cultural function. A fan wrote: 'Detroit's on my bucket list now, mate' — the novels make Detroit legible and desirable to outsiders.
Hypermasculine Performance 4 / 5
Marine sniper, former cop, physically formidable. But also reads Spanish poetry (Lorca, Neruda, Octavio Paz), renovates houses with his own hands, maintains complex romantic relationships, cooks. Masculinity is multidimensional — warrior and builder, killer and reader. Named after Octavio Paz.

Total score: 21 / 25 (5 criteria scored)
