Biography

Victor Stone is the son of scientists Silas and Elinore Stone, both employed at S.T.A.R. Labs in Detroit. Used as a test subject for intelligence enhancement experiments by his parents, Victor's resentment grew until an interdimensional accident killed his mother and destroyed most of his body. Silas rebuilt his son without consent using experimental cybernetics and Mother Box technology. In the New 52 and subsequent continuities, all three Cyborg solo series are set in Detroit, where Victor confronts the irreconcilable tension between the technology that saved him and the father who imposed it, between being DC's most prominent Black hero and being perceived as a weapon. David Walker's run (2015-16) explicitly engaged Black Lives Matter, Detroit's corporate renewal, and the question: 'If you're a black superhero, how do you fight for law and order?' Walker was told by DC editorial to make Cyborg more 'thugged out' — he refused and left the book prematurely.

Timeline

  1. 1980 First appearance in DC Comics Presents #26. Victor Stone becomes Cyborg after his father rebuilds him with experimental technology. — New York City (original origin)
  2. 2015 Cyborg Vol. 1 #1 by David Walker and Ivan Reis. First solo series, set at S.T.A.R. Labs Detroit. Opened with protests outside the facility. Launched at 45,187 copies. — Detroit, MI
  3. 2016 Cyborg Vol. 2 by John Semper Jr. begins. Trade paperback Vol. 2 titled 'Danger in Detroit.' Series runs 23 issues before cancellation. — Detroit, MI
  4. 2017 Justice League film released. Ray Fisher as Cyborg. $661.3 million worldwide gross. Snyder called Cyborg 'the heart of the movie.' — Gotham City (film setting, not Detroit)
  5. 2023 Cyborg Vol. 3: Homecoming by Morgan Hampton (Milestone Initiative graduate). Victor returns to Detroit after his father's death. Tech company Solace threatens to turn 'the Motor City into an overclocked engine for revolutionary artificial intelligence.' — Detroit, MI

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 3 / 5
Works within institutional structures — Justice League member, Teen Titans, S.T.A.R. Labs employee's son. But his body was rebuilt WITHOUT HIS CONSENT by his father Silas Stone. David Walker's run explicitly addressed government attempts to claim ownership of cybernetically enhanced beings — an allegory for state control over Black bodies. His relationship to the institution that made him is fraught.
Community Authorization 4 / 5
Three solo series set in Detroit, each depicting him protecting the city. Walker grounded him in Detroit's community through civilian relationships (Sebastian Cardona, protests outside S.T.A.R. Labs). The 2023 Cyborg: Homecoming series is literally named for his return to the city after his father's death. But Cyborg also operates at Justice League scale, diluting local rootedness.
Violence as Language 5 / 5
His body IS a weapon — sonic cannon, technopathic interface, superhuman strength, Mother Box technology. He didn't choose this. His father weaponized him to save his life. The violence is built into his flesh. Every fight is a reminder of what was taken from him. The technology that makes him powerful is the same technology that destroyed the person he was.
Cultural Preservation Function 3 / 5
Walker deliberately used Detroit's urban renewal narrative — 'urban centers that have fallen on decay and are now being reclaimed by corporations.' The Semper run's trade paperback was literally titled 'Danger in Detroit.' The 2023 Homecoming series made tech-industry gentrification of Detroit a central plot element. But Cyborg's cultural function is more about Black identity in technology than about Detroit specifically.
Hypermasculine Performance 5 / 5
Former high school football star. His body is literally enhanced beyond human capacity — half visible machinery. He is the most physically imposing figure in the SHV batch. But Walker fought DC editorial to let Victor appear fully human sometimes. Walker revealed he was told Cyborg needed more skin showing so he could have tattoos and be 'thugged out.' The tension between the man and the machine IS the hypermasculine performance — forced into a body that performs power he didn't ask for.

Total score: 20 / 25 (5 criteria scored)
