Biography

Curtis 'Curt' Metcalf is a Black genius inventor from Dakota City whose brilliance was discovered at age 12 by industrialist Edwin Alva. Enrolled in elite schools, graduated at 14, accumulated six college degrees, then went to work for Alva Industries, where his inventions generated billions in revenue. When Curtis requested royalties, Alva's response was devastating: 'You are not family. You are an employee. You are a cog in the machine.' Curtis discovered his contract was ironclad and that Alva was laundering drug money and manufacturing illegal weapons. He reported everything to the FBI and SEC. Nothing happened. So Curtis built the Hardware armor from Alva's own corporate resources and launched a one-man guerrilla war against his employer. The series' defining passage — the parakeet metaphor from Hardware #1 — describes a bird repeatedly crashing into a window it cannot perceive: 'The lesson is clear: escape is impossible until one perceives all of the barriers. My name is Curtis Metcalf. But you can call me Hardware.'

Timeline

  1. 1993 First appearance in Hardware #1, 'Angry Black Man.' First Milestone Comics title ever published. Curtis builds the Hardware armor and begins guerrilla war against Edwin Alva. — Dakota City (Detroit analog)
  2. 1997 Hardware #50 — final issue of original Milestone run. — Dakota City
  3. 2021 Hardware: Season One #1-6 (Brandon Thomas/Denys Cowan) relaunches the character in Milestone Returns. Origin updated — Curtis framed by Alva, on the run from Dakota police after the Big Bang. — Dakota City
  4. 2023 Icon vs. Hardware #1-5 (Reginald Hudlin). Hardware seeks to use time travel to alter history for Black people, pitting him against Icon. — Dakota City

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 5 / 5
Built his armor from stolen corporate technology to wage guerrilla war against his employer Edwin Alva. When he reported Alva's crimes through legal channels — FBI, SEC — the system did nothing. His vigilantism is a direct response to institutional failure. In the 2021 revival he is actively fugitive from Dakota police, framed by Alva. The first issue's title — 'Angry Black Man' — declares the posture.
Community Authorization 4 / 5
Protects Dakota broadly, but his conflict is initially personal — Metcalf vs. Alva. His authorization comes from the community recognizing that Alva Industries exploits everyone. The 2021 revival by Brandon Thomas deepens community ties. But Hardware is more isolated than Static — a lone operator with genius-level resources, not a neighborhood kid. His parakeet metaphor resonated beyond comics as a universal statement about Black experience in corporate America.
Violence as Language 5 / 5
Omnicannon, Plasma Whip, PLASER that raises targets to 10,000 degrees. Early Hardware killed dozens of Alva's guards — closer to the Punisher than Superman. McDuffie deliberately opened the series with 'Angry Black Man' as the title. The violence is constitutive, not incidental. Hardware's rage at exploitation IS the character. His arc moves from indiscriminate vengeance toward principled resistance, but the violence never stops being central.
Cultural Preservation Function 4 / 5
The series preserves and transmits knowledge of how corporate exploitation of Black genius operates — the parakeet metaphor has been called 'one of the greatest monologues in the history of comics.' Milestone itself was a cultural preservation act: a Black-owned comics company retaining 100% creative control and all copyrights. Hardware #1 was the first title published. Brandon Thomas's 2021 revival explicitly honors McDuffie's legacy.
Hypermasculine Performance 4 / 5
Genius-level intellect in a battle suit — six college degrees, nano-technological armor, one-man army. The armor is hypermasculine performance literalized: technology as physical dominance. But Curtis is also depicted as emotionally volatile, capable of deep regret over early killings, and evolving from vengeance toward justice. Not Iron Man — 'the exploited worker becomes the hero.'

Total score: 22 / 25 (5 criteria scored)
