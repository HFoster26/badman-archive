Biography

Milton R. Henry (Gaidi Obadele, 1919-2006) and Richard Bullock Henry (Imari Obadele, 1930-2010) were brothers from Philadelphia who became the architects of Detroit's most radical Black Power organizational infrastructure. As a paired unit, they compressed an entire ideological evolution from reformist civil rights to revolutionary separatism into six years, all rooted in Detroit's west-side Linwood corridor. Milton was the public-facing legal and organizational arm — a Tuskegee Airman, Yale-educated attorney, elected official, and the man who recorded Malcolm X's most important speeches. Richard was the intellectual engine — the theorist, author, and strategic mind who wrote the philosophical architecture for Black sovereignty and the modern reparations movement. Their organizational trajectory — GOAL (1962), Freedom Now Party (1963), Malcolm X Society (1967), Republic of New Afrika (1968) — produced the most significant Black nationalist formation to emerge from Detroit. The New Bethel Baptist Church incident of March 29, 1969, when Detroit police fired over 100 rounds into an RNA anniversary gathering, became a defining confrontation that reshaped the city's politics and demonstrated the PRM sequence with textbook clarity: political action met by state violence forcing the militant posture.

Timeline

  1. 1942 Milton Henry serves as a Tuskegee Airman. At Selfridge Field near Detroit, he enters the whites-only officers' club and demands his rights; is court-martialed.
  2. 1950 Milton Henry moves to Pontiac, Michigan after graduating from Yale Law School. Establishes law practice and wins election as Pontiac city commissioner.
  3. 1953 Richard Henry moves to Detroit. Works as reporter for the Michigan Chronicle (1953-1956) and later as technical writer at U.S. Army TACOM.
  4. 1962 Milton Henry, Richard Henry, and Rev. Albert B. Cleage Jr. co-found the Group on Advanced Leadership (GOAL), headquartered at 11605 Linwood Avenue, Detroit.
  5. 1963 Year of confrontation: Richard Henry refuses to send his son to school over racist textbooks, forcing school board reforms. GOAL co-organizes protests against police killing of Cynthia Scott. Henry brothers and Cleage co-found the Michigan Freedom Now Party. November 9-10: Milton Henry and Grace Lee Boggs organize the Grassroots Leadership Conference at King Solomon Baptist Church, where Malcolm X delivers 'Message to the Grassroots,' recorded by Milton's Afro-American Recording Company.
  6. 1964 April 12: GOAL hosts Malcolm X at King Solomon Baptist Church. Morning press conference at GOAL HQ with Malcolm and Milton Henry. Evening: Malcolm delivers 'The Ballot or the Bullet,' thanking 'Milton and Richard Henley' by name. Milton Henry runs for U.S. Representative on Freedom Now Party ticket.
  7. 1965 February 14: Milton Henry organizes Malcolm X's final Detroit appearance at Ford Auditorium. Malcolm's home had been firebombed that morning; Milton arranges for a doctor at the Statler Hilton. Henry records 'The Last Message.' One week later, Malcolm is assassinated. Milton Henry serves as pallbearer at the funeral.
  8. 1967 Brothers found the Malcolm X Society in Detroit to carry forward Malcolm's political philosophy, specifically his advocacy for Black self-determination and nationhood. The Society becomes the direct organizational vehicle for founding the RNA.
  9. 1968 March 29-31: The Malcolm X Society and GOAL convene the Black Government Conference at the Shrine of the Black Madonna (7625 Linwood Street). Over 500 Black nationalists attend. Approximately 100 sign a Declaration of Independence from the United States. Republic of New Afrika provisional government elected: Robert F. Williams (President), Gaidi Obadele/Milton Henry (First Vice President), Betty Shabazz (Second Vice President), Imari Obadele/Richard Henry (Minister of Information). Queen Mother Audley Moore is first signatory.
  10. 1969 March 29: RNA first anniversary celebration at New Bethel Baptist Church (8430 Linwood). Approximately 142 people inside when police confront armed Black Legion bodyguards escorting Milton Henry to his car. Gunfire erupts — who fires first remains disputed. Detroit police fire at least 100 rounds into the church. All 142 people arrested. March 30: Judge George W. Crockett Jr. sets up temporary courtroom at police station, conducts habeas corpus hearings, releases approximately 130 detainees. Political aftermath: 200,000 sign impeachment petition against Crockett; Black United Front of 60 organizations and 3,000 demonstrators defend him. Three men charged; defense attorneys Kenneth Cockrel, Justin Ravitz, and Milton Henry win acquittals in all cases.
  11. 1971 Brothers diverge. Imari Obadele leads RNA faction south to Mississippi to acquire land. Milton Henry stays in Detroit area, distances from militant trajectory, eventually enters seminary and founds Christ Presbyterian Church in Southfield.

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 5 / 5
Milton Henry graduated from Yale Law School, practiced in American courts for decades, then signed a Declaration of Independence rejecting American sovereignty entirely. He was court-martialed for integrating a whites-only officers' club at Selfridge Field. Richard Henry encouraged draft resistance at age 18, refused to send his son to school over racist textbooks, and authored War in America: The Malcolm X Doctrine, framing Black Americans as a subjugated nation with the legal right to secede. Together they co-founded the Republic of New Afrika, drafting an actual constitution (Code of Umoja), claiming five Southern states as national territory, demanding $400 billion in reparations, and establishing a provisional government with ministers and a military arm.
Community Authorization 5 / 5
Milton Henry won election as a Pontiac city commissioner and earned the nickname 'The Black Defender' through decades of criminal defense advocacy. After the 1969 New Bethel incident, a Black United Front of nearly 60 organizations coalesced to defend the detainees and Judge Crockett, and over 3,000 people demonstrated in support. The RNA founding conference drew over 500 Black nationalists with approximately 100 signatories. GOAL's 1963 textbook campaign mobilized the threat of 10,000 protesters and forced the Detroit school board to commission supplemental materials. Rev. C.L. Franklin rented New Bethel for RNA events. Rosa Parks was a friend of the brothers and attended the RNA founding. Malcolm X thanked the brothers by name from the podium.
Violence as Language 4 / 5
The brothers' first moves were always political and intellectual — legal briefs, organizing, speeches, electoral campaigns. The state responded with violence: court-martial, police firing over 100 rounds into New Bethel Baptist Church during the RNA's first anniversary celebration. The Detroit Commission on Community Relations found no evidence anyone inside the church fired on police. Police shot four unarmed people inside, beat and kicked prisoners, and shouted racial epithets. The RNA's armed self-defense posture (Black Legion bodyguard unit) was defensive, inherited from Robert F. Williams' philosophy. After 1969, Milton Henry withdrew from the militant trajectory, entering seminary and founding a church. The system forced the militant posture; the brothers did not initiate violence.
Cultural Preservation Function 5 / 5
Milton Henry's Afro-American Recording Company recorded and posthumously released Malcolm X's three most important Detroit speeches: 'Message to the Grassroots' (1963), 'The Ballot or the Bullet' (1964), and 'The Last Message' (1965). Without these recordings, foundational texts of Black radical thought might not exist in primary audio form. Richard Henry authored War in America: The Malcolm X Doctrine (1968), Revolution and Nation-Building (1970), and Foundations of the Black Nation (1975, House of Songhay, Detroit). Political scientist Robert C. Smith called Imari Obadele 'the father of the modern reparations movement.' GOAL, the Freedom Now Party, the Malcolm X Society, and the RNA each functioned as schools of Black political consciousness. Richard Henry's 1963 textbook campaign forced the Detroit school board to commission 'The Struggle for Freedom and Rights,' sparking nationwide textbook reform activism.
Hypermasculine Performance 5 / 5
Milton Henry embodied multiple registers of Black masculine heroism: Tuskegee Airman (warrior credential), Yale-educated attorney ('The Black Defender'), man who punched a white bus driver in segregated Alabama and stormed a whites-only officers' club. His white Cadillac convertible escorted by armed Black Legion bodyguards performed sovereign masculinity. Richard Henry performed intellectual and revolutionary masculinity — the theorist who named himself after a 14th-century emperor (Abubakari II of Mali), went to federal prison rather than abandon the nation-building project, and claimed patriarchal founding authority with the 'I' suffix. Together they performed sovereign Black manhood — men who answered to no American authority, named themselves in African languages, declared a nation, and stood in the legal and physical crosshairs of the state without yielding.

Total score: 24 / 25 (5 criteria scored)
