Biography

Moses 'Doc' Baldwin was a Detroit Police Department officer who co-founded The Guardians of Michigan in 1963, building the institutional architecture through which Black officers fought racism inside the very department they served. A Miller High School basketball star (class of 1947) from Black Bottom, Baldwin joined the DPD in 1952 when fewer than 150 African Americans served on the approximately 5,000-member force. He spent eleven years reading the system before founding the Guardians — a statewide organization of Black law enforcement officers that grew to over 250 members. The Guardians' anti-STRESS campaign of 1971-1973 helped elect Coleman Young as Detroit's first Black mayor, abolish the city's most lethal police unit, and lay groundwork for the National Black Police Association. Baldwin retired from the DPD in 1978 after twenty-six years and became an investigator for the Detroit Public Schools. He died on March 30, 2020, at age 90 — weeks before George Floyd's murder made police reform a national conversation his organizing had prefigured by half a century.

Timeline

  1. 1947 Graduated from Miller High School as a basketball star. Alumni include Coleman Young, Charles Diggs Jr., Della Reese, Milt Jackson. — Detroit, MI
  2. 1952 Joined the Detroit Police Department. Fewer than 150 Black officers on ~5,000-member force. — Detroit, MI
  3. 1963 Co-founded The Guardians of Michigan, a statewide advocacy organization of Black law enforcement officers. — Detroit, MI
  4. 1969 Guardians publicly defended Judge George Crockett after the New Bethel Baptist Church incident, praising his 'courage and brilliance' and denouncing DPD actions. — Detroit, MI
  5. 1971 Guardians join State of Emergency Committee after STRESS officers kill Ricardo Buck and Craig Mitchell. Off-duty Black officers serve as marshals at 5,000-person protest march from Masonic Temple to Kennedy Square. — Detroit, MI
  6. 1972 Guardians issue formal demand for STRESS abolition (March 9). Baldwin: 'We demand the elimination of STRESS as of today.' Guardians host national Black Police Officers Conference in Detroit (April). National Black Police Association chartered seven months later (November). — Detroit, MI
  7. 1973 Guardians join United Black Coalition demanding STRESS abolition. Actively campaign against DPD Commissioner John Nichols in mayoral race. Coleman Young elected November 1973. — Detroit, MI
  8. 1978 Retired from DPD after approximately 26 years. Became investigator for Detroit Public Schools. — Detroit, MI
  9. 2020 Died March 30 at age 90. DPD Assistant Chief J. White presented plaque of appreciation. Detroit Police escort at viewing. — Detroit, MI

Five-Criteria Evaluation

Each figure is scored against the five criteria that define the badman tradition. Scores range 1–5.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 3 / 5
Wore the DPD badge for 26 years but wielded it against the institution from inside. Founded the Guardians explicitly to oppose the DPOA and the DPD's racial architecture. Defended Judge Crockett against his own department after the New Bethel incident. Called STRESS 'genocide' while still on the force. The badge was the weapon, not the allegiance.
Community Authorization 5 / 5
Over 250 Black officers in the Guardians. 5,000-person anti-STRESS protest march with Guardians members serving as marshals. Co-signatory of United Black Coalition. Coalition with NAACP, ACLU, SCLC. Contributed to founding the National Black Police Association. DPD plaque of appreciation presented posthumously. Ron Scott explicitly acknowledged the Guardians' historical role.
Violence as Language 5 / 5
Twenty-six years as a Detroit Police Department officer — one of the most violent police departments in America during the 1950s-70s. Policed during the Big Four era, the 1967 Rebellion, and the STRESS period. Trained for and deployed physical force as a sworn officer. Additionally wielded institutional force (Guardians vs. DPOA), rhetorical force (naming STRESS 'murder squad,' 'assassination squad,' 'genocide'), legal force (federal lawsuits alongside NAACP), and protective force (24-hour bodyguard for Lt. George Bennett).
Cultural Preservation Function 4 / 5
Built institutional infrastructure (the Guardians) that preserved knowledge of how the DPD's racial machinery operated and transmitted that knowledge across generations. Mentored Roosevelt Lawrence Jr., who wrote Blacks That Wore The Blue (2017). Contributed to founding the National Black Police Association. Hosted national Black police conference in Detroit (April 1972). But no published writings or sustained media presence beyond organizational work.
Hypermasculine Performance 3 / 5
Miller High School basketball star. DPD officer for 26 years — the physical presence and authority of the badge. Played on DPD community basketball team. But masculinity expressed through institutional leadership and collective organizing rather than individual physical dominance.

Organizational / Contemporary

  • Gaidi & Imari Obadele (1969–1973) T2

    The Guardians publicly defended Judge Crockett after the New Bethel Baptist Church incident (an RNA event), placing them in implicit solidarity with the RNA's right to assemble. Both organizations were members of the State of Emergency Committee (September 1971).

  • Ron Scott (1971–1973) T2

    Overlapping anti-STRESS activism. Ron Scott explicitly acknowledged the Guardians' historical role in his organizing manual How to End Police Brutality. Both were active in the anti-STRESS coalition, though from different organizational positions (Guardians inside the department, Scott as former Panther outside it).

  • General Gordon Baker Jr. (1971–1972) T3

    Indirect connection through Kenneth Cockrel as bridging figure. Both the Guardians and the League of Revolutionary Black Workers participated in anti-STRESS coalition activities. Cockrel co-founded the LRBW and led the State of Emergency Committee that the Guardians joined.
