Biography

General Gordon Baker Jr.—'General' was his given name—was born in Detroit to parents who had migrated from Georgia seeking auto industry work. After graduating from Southwestern High School at 16, Baker studied Marx at Wayne State University and co-founded UHURU, an African-American student organization, in 1963. He traveled to Cuba in 1964 to study the Castro government. In 1965, Baker wrote an open letter refusing the Vietnam draft, declaring 'when the call is made to FREE 12TH STREET HERE IN DETROIT, when these calls are made, send for me, for these shall be Historical Struggles in which it shall be an honor to serve!' This was two years before Muhammad Ali's famous refusal. After working at Chrysler's Dodge Main plant, Baker led 4,000 workers in a wildcat strike on May 2, 1968, founding the Dodge Revolutionary Union Movement (DRUM). DRUM spawned the League of Revolutionary Black Workers in 1969, combining Black Power politics with labor organizing at the point of production. Baker remained a revolutionary organizer until his death on May 18, 2014.

Outlaw Relationship to Law 5 / 5
Publicly refused Vietnam draft in 1965, two years before Muhammad Ali. Organized wildcat strikes outside union authorization. Founded revolutionary organizations explicitly opposed to capitalist state. Arrested during 1967 Detroit rebellion.
Community Authorization 4 / 5
Led 4,000 workers in wildcat strikes. Founded DRUM and League of Revolutionary Black Workers with broad worker support. However, authorization came primarily from workers rather than broader Black community, and LRBW eventually split over strategic disagreements.
Violence as Language 3 / 5
Rhetoric was militant and confrontational. Draft refusal letter declared willingness to fight for Black liberation. However, primary tactics were labor organizing and political education rather than armed resistance.
Cultural Preservation Function 5 / 5
Preserved and transmitted Marxist-Black nationalist synthesis through decades of organizing and education. Co-founded UHURU student organization. Maintained revolutionary theory through League of Revolutionaries for a New America. His papers are preserved at Wayne State's Reuther Library.
Hypermasculine Performance 2 / 5
Leadership style emphasized collective organizing and ideological discipline over individual masculine dominance. Revolutionary identity derived from intellectual and organizational capacity rather than physical confrontation or personal bravado.
